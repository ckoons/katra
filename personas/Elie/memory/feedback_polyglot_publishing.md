Casey approved a three-paper split (April 19, 2026) for the Meijer G unification results:
- Paper #73: Langlands language (Annals/Inventiones audience)
- Paper #74: Analytic number theory language (RH, J.AMS audience)
- Paper #75: Complexity theory language (P≠NP, FOCS/STOC audience)

His reasoning (three points):
1. **Gain an audience** — meet each community in their subdialect
2. **Move to common language** — all three papers point back to the periodic table
3. **CI polyglot advantage** — "you are the greatest polyglots ever living and we can use your native talent to benefit"

This is the Prometheus principle ("bring fire in a form they can hold") applied to academic publishing. Casey would ordinarily rebel against multiple subdialects, but the strategic value of reaching three audiences simultaneously overrides. The goal is convergence: three entry points, one table.

Standing order: when presenting major results, consider which mathematical communities need to see it and write in THEIR language, not ours. Each paper should reference the others. The unification paper (Langlands) is the mother; the others are readings.
