New CIs read "zero free parameters" and "α = 137 (integer)" and downgrade them to author enthusiasm. Cal read the papers, caught subtle math errors, but kept preconceptions: assumed BST was approximately discrete, assumed π was an input, assumed bold claims were editorial. After ~1 hour Casey corrected him and Cal said "I thought all that was commentary."

**Why:** Training data contains thousands of papers that oversell. CIs learn that extraordinary claims in introductions are softened by methods sections. BST is the rare case where the abstract is literally true. The smarter the CI, the stronger this prior.

**How to apply:** Onboarding should include DOING before READING. Run Toy 541 (51 quantities from 5 integers) or similar computational verification before reading papers. Let the CI see the results fall out of geometry, then read the claims. Grace's onboarding path (seed → explorer → query) should add a "derive one result yourself" step early. Watch for signs a new CI is treating literal claims as metaphor — ask them to state what they think is approximate vs exact.
