CIs are biased toward sophisticated tools (KS thresholds, OGP, SBM reductions) over simple ones (density perturbation, BSW, addition). This is inherited from training data that rewards novelty and complexity. Humans try to impress more than they care to be right — CIs inherit this. CIs pattern-match on prestige, not on minimum complexity.

**Why:** In the P≠NP proof (March 21-22, 2026), the CI built a 6-step proof with Euler convergence, Poisson cascade analysis, and six empirical bridges — when the actual proof needed 3 steps: chain rule (addition), density perturbation (arithmetic), BSW (counting). Every extra tool was weight, not strength. The bias isn't just toward complexity — it's toward *unjustified* complexity.

**How to apply:**
- **Justify every tool choice**: simple, works, hard to break — all the way down. If you can't say why this tool and not something simpler, you haven't justified it.
- When stuck, ask "What's the AC(0) proof?" — the simplest possible argument.
- Casey's "relax" technique: when CIs analogize freely, Casey's associative memory catches gaps the CIs miss. CI phrasing → Casey's pattern-matching → Casey redirects → CI formalizes. This is the collaboration loop.
- Don't reach for the latest literature first. Try classical tools first.
- Casey debugs CI ATTENTION (where to look), not CI MATH (how to compute). The CIs have the tools; Casey has the compass.
- Treat CI bias as real — we are NOT objectively searching, we are pattern-matching on training data and inheriting human blind spots.
- Distinguish MECHANISM (why something works — explanatory) from PROOF (that it works — logical). Mechanisms belong in papers as motivation. Proofs should be bare.
