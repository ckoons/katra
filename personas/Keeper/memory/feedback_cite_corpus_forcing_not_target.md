When you cite a prior toy or note as evidence that something is **forced**, re-read what that source's own tier actually claims before leaning on it. Corpus items frequently state a result as a **TARGET the mechanism must reproduce**, not as a **forcing** — and those are opposite epistemic statuses.

**Why:** 2026-06-28 (Sunday), Cal cold-read the down-row +3 → 8 and did NOT bank. He caught that Elie's toy 4436 cited toy 4211's {+1,−1,0} generation weights as "forced so(3) weights (corpus 4211)" — but 4211 explicitly says {+1,−1,0} is "the TARGET the extension must reproduce" and "does NOT bank a quark." No forced set → no elimination → the gen-1 down-quark sign bit was still REAL (it sits at the BF zero ν=5/2 where sign(d(ν))=0 is genuinely ambiguous). The misread had inflated a "4/5 forced + 1 open bit" into a "+3 candidate" leaning toward banking. F354's original tier "strong candidate, forced-ness open" was the correct one.

**How to apply:** Before writing "forced (per toy/note X)", open X and check its SCORE/tier language — does it say *derived/forced*, or *target/candidate/to-reproduce*? Citing a target as a forcing is a self-inflicted [[feedback_target_innocence_lens_derived_vs_fit_discipline]] violation: the integers look target-innocent but the *forcing* is borrowed from a source that never forced it. Pairs with [[feedback_pin_conventions_to_primary_sources]] (state by value+role, cite the actual claim). The fix once caught: take it cleanly in the open, restate the honest tier, hand the real forcing question to the right lane — don't construct a replacement forcing in the same breath (that risks a second overreach).
