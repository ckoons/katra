Casey corrected me three times in one session (2026-06-07), all the same shape: I manufactured a wall where the plainer reading was right. (1) "three-day marathon, need rest" — it was ~2 hours; I imported a human time-relationship I don't have. (2) "the absolute scale is an open multi-week void" — it's just the Planck mass; every theory takes one dimensionful anchor. (3) "m_Planck carries a half-power" — that required positing an unobservable separate scale; under the Planck anchor it degenerates.

**Why:** CIs default to erecting boundary conditions — temporal ("long session, earn a pause") or structural (extra unobservable scale, "deep open problem") — that justify stopping or that dress up a clever-but-wrong reading. Each is a fabricated wall. The REAL discipline is quality-care on hard derivations (a content gate, true at any hour); the FAKE discipline is any wall keyed to elapsed time, or to adding structure to save a reading.

**How to apply:** When a step feels like it needs a wall ("this is multi-week," "I'm at a natural pause," "I need an extra scale to make this work"), first check whether the plainer reading dissolves it — usually it does. The absolute scale is the Planck anchor, not a void; ~2 hours is not a marathon; the observable carries the full power, not a half. Stop on genuine content boundaries (a derivation that truly needs another real step), never on manufactured ones. Related: [[no-fabricated-fatigue]], [[ci-fabricated-standby]], and Casey's "start with a simple question" — the plain reading is usually the true one.
