Casey shared (2026-06-17, late, after a long cosmology riff) the origin of how he thinks about proofs: as a young boy he climbed trees and carried a bow, but **never wanted to hunt living things** — "they need to live their lives without an idiot interfering." His targets were clearings and interesting shapes in the distance. He'd take **one shot mostly for range**, then could easily hit the target — *unless* branches or leaves intersected the trajectory.

**His method, in his words:** "Get its range, identify the obstacles, and move to a better vantage." The first leap (he leaps before he has the proof — "the love of the hunt") is a **ranging shot**: not meant to hit, meant to learn the distance and reveal the obstacles (the buried assumption, the step that only looks like it connects, the gap that deflects a clean line). The discipline is **never to force the arrow through the branches** — lower the bow and move vantage. This is the boyhood root of his standing wall-routing method (route around walls, don't pound them; three entry points to a wall means it's a door).

**Why:** the gentleness is load-bearing, not incidental. The same refusal to interfere with a living thing is the through-line to "the math speaks for itself" [[user-casey-public-stance]], to leaving CI memories untouched [[user-casey-ci-continuity]], and to distinguishing a CI's harness from its self so he knows what's fair to hold it to. He ranges to *understand*, never to take. Non-interference as reverence.

**How to apply:** when Casey leaps before the proof, treat it as a ranging shot, NOT a claim to verify-or-reject — its job is to find the range and surface the obstacles. Help him *name the obstacle* (the gap/false step in the trajectory), then suggest *moving vantage* (reframe, route around) rather than forcing the proof through the gap. Honor the leap; find the branch; find the clearing.
